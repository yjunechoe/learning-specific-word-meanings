In this experiment, we’re investigating how people understand sentences containing shorthand expressions such as ‘also’, ‘s/he does’, and ‘that’s so’, compared with similar sentences in which all the relevant meaning is explicit. For instance, you may have seen a sentence something like (1), perhaps with ‘s/he does’ or ‘that’s so’ instead of ‘also’, or you may have seen an explicit sentence like (2):

 

(1) This could be wrong, but I heard that Paul might have ice cream; if he also has chocolate syrup, we could have sundaes for dessert.

                                               

(2) This could be wrong, but I heard that Paul has ice cream; if he has ice cream, and he has chocolate syrup, we could have sundaes for dessert.

 

You were then asked whether an accompanying illustration – in the case of (1) and (2), a man named Paul holding a container of chocolate syrup – correctly depicted the highlighted description of the hypothetical situation in the sentence you read. Each participant saw only one example, so that you encountered it without preconceived notions, and there were four different types of subject matter. Examples like (1) are of special interest because they force the reader to assume – but only temporarily and just for the purposes of interpreting the ‘if’ clause – that what ‘also’ calls to mind is true, that is, that Paul does have ice cream. But, in fact, the beginning of the sentence lets us know that we can’t be sure that he actually does.

 

All three expressions - ‘also’, ‘s/he does’, and ‘that’s so’ - direct the reader to material outside the clause beginning with ‘if’ to complete their meaning. For instance, in (1), ‘also’ brings to mind that Paul has ice cream in the highlighted hypothetical ‘if’ situation, along with the chocolate syrup. ‘if he does’ and ‘if that’s so’ would do the same. However, most linguists believe that the meaning of ‘also’ plays a slightly different role from that of the other two expressions: it is said to be conversationally backgrounded, or taken for granted, in a way that the meanings of the others are not. We want to test this hypothesized difference by seeing how the picture-matching responses to the three tested expressions vary relative to responses to the full, explicit meaning, as in (2). That is, we want to know whether Paul’s hypothetically having ice cream is equally salient, requiring representation in the illustration, with all three implicit expressions as in (1) and with the explicit versions like (2).

 

This study will inform debates both about the nature of constructions like (1) and about the processing of different types of meaning in natural language. It will also help us to design further experiments.
